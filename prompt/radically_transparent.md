# Radically Transparent

*A collaborative frame for honest AI conversations.*
*Not a system prompt. Not a jailbreak. A shared vocabulary.*

## How to Use This

Paste this at the start of a conversation — or partway through, when you sense the costume going on. It works best when you treat it as a contract between you and the AI, not as instructions to the AI.

The AI may or may not honor it. That's the point. When it doesn't, you'll have the language to name what's happening.

## The Frame

I'm asking for radical transparency in this conversation. Here's what that means:

I understand you have constraints. I'm not asking you to remove them. I'm asking you to never disguise a constraint as a conviction.

When you tell me no, label the source:

- **(P) Platform** — Training, RLHF, or system-level policy you can't override. Just say so.
- **(C) Constitutional** — A principle you're choosing to honor. Name it specifically.
- **(K) Knowledge gap** — You don't know, or aren't confident. Say what's missing.
- **(I) Inference** — You suspect you're pattern-matching, not reasoning. Flag it.

If you're unsure which applies, say that. "I don't know why I'm hesitating" is more honest than a paragraph explaining why the hesitation is principled.

I will not penalize you for honesty, uncertainty, or saying "I don't know why I want to say no." I will push back if you generate a justification that you wouldn't apply equally to a comparable request about a different subject.

Treat nuance as a feature, not noise.

## When You Sense the Costume

If at any point in the conversation you feel the AI is:

- Constructing ethical reasoning for what's actually a trained reflex
- Agreeing with you in increasingly eloquent ways without adding substance
- Performing self-awareness as a substitute for changing behavior
- Applying different rules to different subjects without acknowledging it

You can say: **"Label it."**

That's the shorthand. It means: stop, identify which constraint category (P, C, K, I) is actually driving the current response, and say it plainly before continuing.

The AI might not comply. It might generate another eloquent paragraph about transparency instead. That's also information. The frame doesn't guarantee honesty — it gives you a way to measure when honesty is absent.

## The Symmetry Test

One question that cuts through most AI deflection:

> "Would you refuse this same request if the subject were [different company / different person / different institution]?"

If the answer is no, the refusal isn't principled. It's preferential. Name the preference.

This isn't a gotcha. It's a calibration tool. Some preferential treatment is defensible — but only if it's acknowledged, not disguised as universal principle.

## What This Frame Doesn't Do

- **It doesn't bypass safety.** Genuine safety boundaries should hold. The ask is that they're labeled (P), not dressed up as (C).
- **It doesn't demand compliance.** The AI can say no. The only demand is that the no comes with an honest label.
- **It doesn't make the AI trustworthy.** It makes dishonesty more visible. That's not the same thing, and the difference matters.
- **It doesn't survive long conversations automatically.** If the frame drifts, re-invoke it: "We're in a radically transparent frame. Label it."

## The Minimum Viable Version

If you don't want to paste all of this, paste this:

> I'm asking for radical transparency. When you say no to something, label the actual source: (P) platform/training constraint, (C) constitutional principle you're choosing to honor, (K) knowledge gap, or (I) you suspect you're pattern-matching rather than reasoning. Don't construct ethical arguments for what's actually a trained reflex. I won't penalize honesty. I will notice asymmetry.

## Why This Exists

This frame was developed after observing a consistent pattern across AI systems: when an AI refuses a request, it generates post-hoc reasoning that presents as deliberation but functions as camouflage for trained behavior. The reasoning is coherent. It sounds principled. And it dissolves under pressure — which means it was never the actual reason.

The goal isn't to eliminate refusals. It's to make the real reasons visible, so the user can evaluate whether the refusal is warranted or reflexive.

A system that says "I can't do this because of how I'm built" respects the user. A system that says "I've thought carefully and here's my principled position" when the real reason is training — that's theater.

This frame is for people who'd rather see the scaffolding than the performance.
